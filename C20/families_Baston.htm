Arborfield Court

 

 

 

John Thomas Baston moved to Arborfield after his discharge from the Army in about 1908. In the 1911 census he was living in a cottage at Arborfield Court with his wife and two children and is described as an Army pensioner (butler).

John was born at Barnack, near Stamford, Lincolnshire in 1874 to an agricultural labourer, George Baston. He joined the Army in 1890, probably lying about his real age, and became a member of the Royal Artillery with the rank of driver and then gunner, both equivalent to private.

In 1897 he married Alice Drage at All Saints Church in Blackheath, London, not far from the Royal Artillery depot at Woolwich. He had two children, Frank Carvely Baston, born in Ulveston near Bristol in 1898, and Hilda Baston, born in 1904 at Woolwich.

According to his surviving service record, he remained in garrisons and camps around the British Isles until November 1899 when his battery, the 63rd Battery of the Royal Field Artillery embarked to South Africa from Birkenhead on the requisitioned transport SS Ismore. However the ship never made it to port, as en-route she ran on to rocks at Columbine Point, a notorious site of shipwrecks. A lighthouse was somewhat belatedly erected in 1936. The men and most of the equipment were rescued, but many of the guns and the 140 horses on board were not so lucky.

The battery was taken by the SS Algeria to Natal where they disembarked and were re-equipped. The 63rd battery took part in operations against the Boers in the Transvaal culminating in the relief of Ladysmith. John Baston was transferred to “A” battery (Chestnut troop) of the Royal Horse Artillery, and stayed in South Africa until December 1900 he and the rest of the troop were sent back to England on the SS Hawarden Castle . He received the Queen's South Africa medal with six clasps recognising the actions in which his units fought including Cape Colony, Tegula Heights, Orange Free State, Relief of Ladysmith, Transvaal and Laing's Nek. He was also awarded the Long Service and Good Conduct Medal in the reign of Edward VII. He returned to South Africa on garrison duties in 1905 until 1907 and was finally discharged to the reserve in 1909.

In the First World War he enrolled as a Special Constable and received the Special Constabulary Long Service medal with clasp for the Great War. In December 1917 he re-enlisted in the army, this time in the Army Service Corps and promoted to acting corporal. He was finally discharged back to the reserve in 1919. This time his address is given as Ashridgewood which is where Maud Allfrey (nee Bruce ) the daughter of Mrs Bruce of Arborfield Court had a house, so perhaps he moved when Arborfield Court was sold in 1916.

During the 1920's John Thomas lived in London, probably still as a butler. However during the final years of his life he was the tenant of the “Hare and Hounds” public house in Old Warden Bedfordshire. The pub is still standing and little changed, at least externally, as it was in his day. On December 7th 1927 he died and is buried in Old Warden churchyard. His headstone also commemorates the death of his only son, Frank, who was killed in the First World War.

Frank Baston was an apprentice with Goodall and Co. , plumbers and decorators with a shop on The Butts in Reading. At the age of 18 he joined the 6th Battalion, Royal Berkshire regiment and went to France to fight on the Western Front in August 1916. Although his service record is missing (probably destroyed by bombing in 1940) he would almost certainly fought in the latter stages of Battle of the Somme during September 1916.

In February 1917 the battalion saw action in a ill fated attack at Boom Ravine where they met their objectives but had no support and had to withdraw. They were then sent an area south of Arras where they took part in an attack near Wancourt. According to the battalion war diary on May 3rd they moved forward through a heavy German barrage and sustained 15 killed, 23 wounded and 2 missing. The following two days were described as “quiet” but patrols were unable to find any wounded. Shell fire killed at least one officer on 5th May although no other ranks are mentioned in the diary entry.

It is not known exactly when Frank was killed and his body was never found but officially his date of death was recorded as 5th May 1917, a few days short of his 19th birthday. He is commemorated on the Arras Memorial in the Faubourg-d'Amiens cemetery in Arras and on the memorial at Arborfield Cross. His family were presented with a 'death penny' to commemorate his war service.

John's employer Maud Allfrey also lost a son, Frederic de Vere Bruce Allfrey , at the beginning of WWI in September 1914.
