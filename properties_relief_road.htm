The effect of the motorways on Arborfield

Previous plans for Bypasses around Arborfield

 

Although there had been rumours that the Army would vacate its site at Arborfield dating back to 1937 when the Remount Depot closed, in fact the Army presence expanded in 1938 when the mechanised RAOC Depot moved to the new Arborfield Garrison, followed by the Army Technical School in 1939. The Second World War and the increasing demands for technical skills led to the formation of the Royal Electrical and Mechanical Engineers (REME) in 1942, and the siting of several barracks at the Garrison. The Army Technical School later became the Army Apprentices' College, among other names; it eventually closed in 2004, leaving the School of Electronic and Aeronautic Engineering (SEAE) .

The Ministry of Defence had been planning to merge all of its more advanced technical training onto one site covering the Army, the Navy and Royal Air Force for some years, and after several false starts, a planning application was submitted to convert the former RAF base at Lyneham, Wiltshire, in June 2013 .

The area around the Garrison had been identified as a possible large housing development by Wokingham Borough Council, and it was thought that if 3,500 dwellings were to be concentrated in one 'Strategic Development Location', then this would pay for the infrastructure needed to support it. In 2005 it was proposed to build a bypass well to the west of Arborfield, crossing over fields near Bartletts and Whites Farms on Swallowfield Road, and then to cross the River Loddon and meet another bypass in Shinfield.The cost proved to be too high for even 3,500 houses to finance, and Wokingham Borough Council finally announced its 'technical recommendation' in July 2013 for a more modest scheme.

A packed meeting at Henry Street Garden Centre on July 15th 2013 was surprised to hear that the recommended route was much closer to Arborfield Cross than had been expected. In fact, for most of its route it was to follow the line of a bypass originally planned in January 1945  [Berkshire Record Office reference C/CL/C3/21/1] but dropped in the late 1960s, by which time the settlement boundary of Arborfield Cross had almost reached the line of this route.

This article explores the land to the west of Arborfield Cross that would be affected by the proposed route. Click on each image to see a larger version.

When footings for a house extension in Melrose Gardens were dug in 1982, to the owners' surprise, they found a high-voltage cable that ran from a sub-station opposite. It was found that the sub-station had never been used, and that the cable was deemed to be redundant. It had been intended to serve a small housing development to the west of Melrose Gardens, bounded by the 1945 bypass.

 Melrose Gardens was developed in the 1960s, but immediately behind it are two much older buildings known as Chamberlain's Farmhouse and Applemore Cottage, as seen here. Both are Grade II Listed.

A little further to the west, on the other side of Swallowfield Road, is another Grade II listed building, Yew Tree Cottage, just behind the trees on the left of this view.

An old footpath still runs from the centre of Arborfield Cross westwards over the ridge where Arborfield Court is located.

It passes between the houses on Melrose Gardens and up a steady slope well-known to small children, because it's an ideal spot for tobogganing in the snow. It's also well-known as the route of the 'Health Walks' held every Thursday.
The footpath continues along the hill ridge alongside Arborfield Court and its Gardeners' Cottages, which would overlook the proposed Relief Road.

This view is from the ridge looking towards Eversley Road with Ducks Nest Farm to the right, and the Garrison Water Tower in the distance on the far left.
